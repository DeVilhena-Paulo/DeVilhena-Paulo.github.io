Proof of Programs with Effect Handlers

de Vilhena's PhD Defense

Abstract

Effect handlers form a powerful programming concept offering a relatively simple interface to delimited control: a program fragment that performs an effect is suspended and reified as a continuation that, when invoked, resumes the paused computation. Although the mechanistic behavior of effect handlers and many interesting applications are well-documented, an important question remains to be addressed: what are the logical principles that govern effect handlers? In particular, how to reason abstractly about a continuation, rather than thinking concretely as a fragment of the stack? Moreover, can we reason separately about a program that performs effects and a program that handles these effects? This thesis answers these questions by introducing Hazel, a Separation Logic for effect handlers, built as an extension of Iris. Hazel introduces a novel specification language by means of which one can describe the behavior of programs, including continuations. The logic allows one to compose specifications in a modular fashion through familiar reasoning rules, such as the bind rule and the frame rule, and novel ones, such as the reasoning rules for handling and performing effects.
